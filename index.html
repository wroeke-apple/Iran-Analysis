💬 US–Iran Talks (Apr–Jun 2025)

Five rounds of indirect talks mediated by Oman — the first substantive engagement since the Trump administration restored maximum pressure in early 2025.

Key Sticking Points

The US demanded zero enrichment on Iranian soil and proposed a regional consortium model. Iran countered that enrichment is an "inalienable right" — a red line that cannot be crossed. Iran's lead negotiator reportedly told US counterparts that its 460kg of 60%-enriched uranium could produce 11 nuclear bombs.

Mediator Framework

Qatar, Turkey, and Egypt proposed a compromise: Iran would commit to zero enrichment for 3 years, then limit to <1.5%, and transfer its enriched stockpile to a third country. Iran rejected the proposal as "unbalanced."

Collapse

US strikes on June 21–22 ended talks indefinitely. Iran suspended all diplomatic engagement on the nuclear file.

💥 June 2025 US Military Strikes

Overnight strikes targeted Iran's three main nuclear sites:

Fordow
Deeply buried enrichment facility. Struck with bunker-buster munitions.
Natanz
Iran's main uranium enrichment complex. Severe damage reported.
Isfahan
Uranium conversion and fuel fabrication site.

IAEA Director Grossi (Feb 2026): "Severe damage, not total damage." Nuclear material remains "in large quantities" but "some less accessible." IAEA inspectors in Iran but cannot access damaged sites.

📋 IAEA & NPT Compliance Status

IAEA Director Grossi warned in early 2026 that he may declare Iran "in non-compliance" with the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty. Iran's Foreign Ministry responded that there is "no established procedure" for inspecting damaged nuclear facilities — effectively blocking international verification.

Bottom line: The strikes set back Iran's weapons timeline but did not eliminate the program. With IAEA access denied, the international community has no verified picture of Iran's current nuclear status. This ambiguity itself is a source of ongoing risk.

🔍 Strategic Assessment

Iran's "Axis of Resistance" was built on the concept of strategic depth — the idea that forward-deployed proxies could deter Israeli or US aggression by threatening multi-front retaliation. This deterrence framework has been fundamentally undermined.

The control paradox: Iran's proxies offered plausible deniability and geopolitical reach, but they also acted on their own logic. Hamas's Oct 7 attack — which Iran likely did not direct — triggered an Israeli campaign that has dismantled much of what Iran spent decades building. The proxies are now "out for themselves," according to Foreign Policy analysis (Mar 2, 2026).

Without sustained Iranian financial and logistical backing — now severely compromised — these actors face rapid weakening, internal fragmentation, or reorientation toward purely local agendas. The collapse of the Axis benefits Israel, Saudi Arabia, and the US.

The power vacuum left by weakened proxies is being filled by local actors and competing regional powers, creating new instability dynamics that may be harder to predict or manage than the previous Iranian-controlled framework.

🏗️ Organizational Structure

The IRGC is not simply a military — it is a parallel state with five functional branches, each controlling a distinct domain of Iranian power:

🪖
Ground Forces
~100,000 troops organized in a corps structure mirroring the regular army. Controls border regions and operates independently of the Artesh (conventional military). Used domestically during protests and externally in Syria and Iraq.
🚀
Aerospace Force
15,000 personnel. Controls Iran's entire ballistic missile arsenal — the strategic deterrence core. Operates ~3,000+ short- and medium-range mobile ballistic missiles. Also controls space launch and drone programs. Was the instrument of October 2023 and April 2024 strikes on Israel.
Severely degraded by June 2025 & Feb 2026 strikes
⚓
Naval Forces
Controls the Persian Gulf and Strait of Hormuz operations — separate from the regular Iranian Navy. Operates fast-attack craft, midget submarines, and maritime drone swarms. The primary mechanism for any Hormuz closure threat. Also conducts seizures of commercial vessels as leverage.
🌐
Quds Force
Elite external operations unit, 2,000–5,000 personnel. The IRGC's "foreign policy arm." Trains, funds, arms, and directs all proxy forces — Hezbollah, Hamas, Houthis, Iraqi militias. Also runs intelligence networks, assassination operations, and arms smuggling routes. Think of it as the CIA, Special Forces, and a foreign aid agency combined.

💰 The IRGC's Economic Empire

The IRGC is often called "a business empire with an army attached." It controls an estimated 10–50% of Iran's entire economy through a deliberately opaque web of foundations, holding companies, and front corporations — many registered under civilian names to mask military ownership.

🏗️ Khatam al-Anbiya Construction HQ

The IRGC's primary economic arm — one of Iran's largest contractors. Described by the US Treasury as controlling "hundreds of companies" across construction, mining, oil, and telecom via 800+ front companies and subcontractors. Key projects include:

→South Pars gas field development (phases 13, 14, 22–24) — the world's largest natural gas field
→Tehran's Imam Khomeini International Airport operations
→Tehran Metro Line 7
→Major dam, refinery, and pipeline contracts across Iran
→Designated by US OFAC in 2006; broadly sanctioned by EU and UK

📡 Telecom & Finance

In 2009, the IRGC-linked Mobin Trust Consortium acquired 51% of Telecommunication Company of Iran — the country's largest telecom provider — for $7.8 billion. This gives the IRGC direct access to Iran's entire phone and internet infrastructure, enabling surveillance and control of communications.

🛢️ Oil & Energy

Beyond South Pars, IRGC entities control significant portions of Iran's petroleum distribution network, smuggling operations for sanctioned oil, and the shadow fleet logistics chain. The IRGC effectively taxes oil exports by inserting itself into the distribution chain.

🏦 Cooperative Foundation (Bonyad-e Ta'avon-e Sepah)

The IRGC's financial holding entity. Controls real estate, banking interests, and manufacturing across consumer goods, pharmaceuticals, and food. Provides financial services to IRGC personnel and their families — creating a self-contained parallel economy.

Why this matters geopolitically: The IRGC's economic empire means sanctions hurt ordinary Iranians disproportionately — IRGC entities have sophisticated workarounds, offshore accounts, and front companies. Conversely, any post-regime transition would require dismantling this empire, which would be fiercely resisted. The economic empire is the IRGC's institutional survival mechanism.

🗳️ Political Power & the Succession Play

How the IRGC Controls Politics

The IRGC's political dominance operates through five overlapping mechanisms:

1.Guardian Council vetting: IRGC-aligned clerics on the Guardian Council block reformist candidates from running, ensuring only ideologically acceptable figures hold elected office
2.Intelligence penetration: IRGC intelligence (separate from the Ministry of Intelligence) monitors government ministries, judiciary, and parliament for disloyalty
3.Economic leverage: Ministries and officials that challenge IRGC contracts or economic interests face obstruction, investigation, or worse
4.Media control: IRGC controls key state media and press outlets, shaping domestic narratives
5.Physical intimidation: The Basij and IRGC intelligence units have a documented history of arresting, torturing, and disappearing political opponents

The Succession Power Grab (Mar 2026)

Following Khamenei's assassination, the IRGC moved immediately. According to Iran International, IRGC commanders began "repeated contacts and psychological and political pressure" on Assembly of Experts members from March 3, 2026 onward — demanding they vote for Mojtaba Khamenei, the late Supreme Leader's son.

This is constitutionally irregular — the Assembly of Experts is nominally an independent clerical body — but reflects the IRGC's calculation that a Khamenei dynasty figure provides the most legitimacy while keeping real power with the Guard.

NBC News and Al Jazeera both reported that "regardless of who's chosen, real power is now likely in the hands of the IRGC" — an assessment shared across Western intelligence services.

Historical parallel: This mirrors Pakistan's dynamic where the ISI and military hold de facto power regardless of who holds formal office — a model that produces persistent instability, not consolidation.

🦅 Trump's "Maximum Pressure Plus" Strategy

The Trump administration has pursued an aggressive, multi-instrument strategy against Iran combining sanctions, tariffs, energy policy, and direct military force — a significant escalation from the "maximum pressure" campaign of the first term.

💸 Sanctions Architecture

Early 2025: Maximum pressure sanctions reimposed targeting Iran's shadow fleet responsible for oil exports. Key new elements: Chinese "teapot" refinery sanctions, OFAC actions against financial facilitators, and coordination with EU partners on snapback mechanisms.

🛢️ Oil Policy as Weapon

The Trump administration coordinated with OPEC+ to maintain a well-supplied oil market (currently ~$55/barrel, down 18% from 2025), deliberately creating an environment where energy-dependent adversaries like Iran, Russia, and Venezuela face maximum fiscal pain.

🌎 Venezuela — The Oil Connection

On January 3, 2026, US forces arrested Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro. Trump declared the US would "take back" Venezuelan oil, with revenue earmarked for the Venezuelan people, US oil companies, and "reimbursement to the United States." This served three strategic goals:

→Disrupted the Iran–Venezuela–China oil triangle, a key Iranian sanctions workaround
→Eliminated a drug trafficking corridor partially used by Iranian-affiliated networks
→Sent a signal about willingness to use direct force to reshape oil markets

The FDD (Foundation for Defense of Democracies) explicitly called for applying the Venezuela blockade model to Iran's oil shipments in December 2025 — a blueprint the administration appeared to follow months later.

🎯 Tariff Leverage

Trump's broad tariff architecture serves dual purposes: generating domestic revenue while creating leverage in negotiations with China (which imports Iranian oil despite sanctions). The threat of expanded tariffs on Chinese goods has complicated Beijing's calculus on how openly to support Iranian oil exports and military needs.

The Grand Strategy: Trump's approach treats Iran, Russia, Venezuela, and China as an interconnected adversary network. By simultaneously targeting each node — sanctions on Iran, military action in Venezuela, tariff pressure on China, military support for Ukraine degrading Russia — the administration is attempting to collapse the economic and political infrastructure that allows these actors to resist US pressure.

🏛️ Congressional & Institutional Context

Trump's State of the Union (early 2026) stated a preference for diplomatic resolution but explicit rejection of a nuclear-armed Iran. US repositioned the USS Abraham Lincoln and accompanying destroyers to the Persian Gulf region in mid-January 2026 — a signal both to Iran and to allies.

🇷🇺 Russia

Rhetoric vs. Capacity

Foreign Minister Lavrov condemned the strikes as "unprovoked military aggression" and offered to help find a diplomatic solution. However, years of grinding war in Ukraine have hollowed out Russia's capacity to project power into the Middle East.

Military Constraints

Russia's military is overstretched. Its Middle East influence, built during the Syria intervention (2015–2024), has collapsed along with the Assad regime. Moscow cannot provide Iran with meaningful military aid without jeopardizing its own operations.

Economic Constraints

Russia's economy is under sustained pressure from Western sanctions. Oil revenue — its primary geopolitical tool — is constrained by the same low-price environment that punishes Iran.

Iran's Tech Dependence

Iran had turned to Russia and China for missile technology and military components after the 2025 12-Day War. With Russia overstretched and under scrutiny, that supply chain is now disrupted.

🇨🇳 China

The Critical Balancing Act

China is Iran's largest trading partner and a key buyer of sanctioned Iranian oil via teapot refineries. It has strong economic incentives to maintain ties with Tehran but equally strong incentives not to directly confront the US.

Condemnation Without Commitment

Beijing condemned the strikes at the UN Security Council but explicitly did not pledge material support. China's restrained stance is consistent with its historical approach: buy Iranian oil quietly, condemn US aggression publicly, avoid direct conflict.

BRI & INSTC Disruption

The strikes on Bandar Abbas port (Iran's main trade hub) threaten the International North-South Transport Corridor and Belt and Road Initiative projects — infrastructure that China and Russia rely on to bypass Western-controlled maritime routes. This economic damage may motivate diplomatic efforts even as military support remains off the table.

Tariff Leverage

Trump's tariff pressure complicates China's ability to openly defy US sanctions on Iran. The risk of secondary sanctions on Chinese entities remains a powerful deterrent against escalating material support.

💸 Root Causes of Collapse

Sanctions & Oil Revenue Loss

Maximum pressure sanctions slashed Iran's oil export revenue — its primary budget source. The shadow fleet workaround, while partially effective, could not compensate for the scale of restriction.

Governance Failures

For the first time, both Khamenei and Pezeshkian publicly acknowledged that governance failures — not just sanctions — were responsible for economic dysfunction. Mismanagement of energy infrastructure led to electricity and gas shortages even in an energy-rich country.

Currency Spiral

The rial has lost approximately 97% of its value since 2018. Each round of sanctions, conflict escalation, or political uncertainty triggers further capital flight and rial depreciation — feeding into import-cost inflation across all sectors.

Strike Amplification

The Feb 2026 strikes added infrastructure destruction to an already fragile economy — power outages, fuel shortages, port disruptions at Bandar Abbas, and a collapse in investor/business confidence.

🔮 Economic Outlook

Near-Term (0–6 months)

Continued currency depreciation, high inflation, and supply disruptions. Succession uncertainty and security instability will prevent any economic stabilization. Foreign investment essentially zero.

Medium-Term (6–24 months)

Outcome depends heavily on the political scenario. A new Supreme Leader willing to negotiate sanctions relief could begin a stabilization path — but any credible deal would require NPT compliance, IAEA access, and enrichment restrictions that the IRGC is unlikely to accept.

Structural Issues

Even in a best-case diplomatic scenario, reversing years of economic damage will take a decade. The rial's credibility is destroyed, institutional capacity is degraded, and a generation of educated Iranians has emigrated (brain drain).

Key watch item: If China significantly expands oil purchases despite secondary sanction risk, it could provide Iran a meaningful economic lifeline — but China's appetite for direct confrontation with the US remains low.

🗺️ The "Donroe Doctrine" — Resource Supremacy Theory

Trump's 2025 National Security Strategy explicitly revives the Monroe Doctrine, extended to what analysts at Middle East Eye and OpenDemocracy have termed the "Donroe Doctrine" — the use of economic and military force to assert US control over energy and critical mineral resources within and beyond the Western Hemisphere.

The core theory: The US is systematically moving to control or deny adversaries access to the world's most strategically valuable resources — oil, rare earths, critical minerals, and maritime chokepoints — before a potential multi-decade rivalry with China reaches its critical phase.

🔗 The Interconnected Move Sequence

Critics and analysts point to a notable sequencing across the Trump administration's moves that suggests coordination rather than improvisation:

Jan 2025

Broad tariffs imposed on China, Canada, EU, Mexico. Economists note this creates maximum negotiating leverage and forces every major player into a defensive posture — preventing coordinated opposition to subsequent moves.
Jan–Mar 2025
Step 2: Energy Independence — "Drill Baby Drill" + OPEC Coordination
US oil production increased and OPEC+ encouraged to maintain oversupply, driving prices to ~$55–60/barrel. This simultaneously: (a) reduces US vulnerability to oil shocks from its own actions, (b) punishes Iran, Russia, and Venezuela whose budgets require $80–100+/barrel to balance, (c) gives Trump geopolitical flexibility.
Apr–Jun 2025
Step 3: Neutralize the Nuclear Wildcard — Iran Talks, then Strikes
Five rounds of nuclear talks offer Iran a genuine off-ramp. Iran's refusal — citing enrichment as an "inalienable right" — provides the political justification for military action. June 2025 strikes destroy Fordow, Natanz, Isfahan. Iran loses its primary deterrence-by-threat mechanism.
Oct–Nov 2025
Step 4: China Trade Truce — Separate Beijing from Tehran
After tariff escalation to 130%, Trump and Xi meet in Busan. Trade truce achieved. China suspends rare earth export restrictions. Analysis: this isolates Iran by reducing China's incentive to actively undermine US pressure, while preserving economic leverage for later use.
Jan 2026
Step 5: Venezuela — Close the Oil Sanctions Workaround
Maduro arrested, US takes operational control of Venezuelan oil infrastructure. Simultaneously eliminates: (a) the Iran-Venezuela-China sanctions evasion triangle, (b) a drug trafficking corridor, (c) Chinese-influenced oil flows in the Western Hemisphere. FDD had explicitly recommended applying the Venezuela blockade model to Iran — which happened weeks later.
Feb 28, 2026

With Iran's nuclear program set back, oil revenue near zero, proxies degraded, China de-linked from active support, and Venezuela closed as a workaround — the US-Israel operation kills Khamenei. The succession crisis and internal pressure are now meant to finish what sanctions and strikes started.
The counter-argument: Many analysts (Responsible Statecraft, CFR) argue this pattern reflects Trump's transactional opportunism rather than a coherent strategy — and that the gaps between stated goals and execution capacity are enormous. Venezuela's oil is not easily "taken back," Greenland has not been acquired, and a post-Khamenei Iran may be more chaotic and dangerous, not more compliant.

💵 The Petrodollar Dimension

A significant thread in the theory-crafting community focuses on the petrodollar system — the arrangement since 1974 by which oil is priced and settled in US dollars, generating global demand for dollar liquidity and underwriting US financial hegemony.

The De-Dollarization Threat

Russia, Iran, Venezuela, and China have been actively building infrastructure to trade oil outside dollar systems — BRICS payment platforms, yuan-denominated oil contracts (petroyuan), and INSTC corridor trade in non-dollar currencies. If these corridors scale, dollar demand falls and US debt financing costs rise.

The US Response Theory

OpenDemocracy and the Institute of Geoeconomics argue the Venezuela intervention and Iran campaign are less about specific oil revenues and more about reasserting that dollar pricing of oil is non-negotiable — by disrupting non-dollar energy corridors and demonstrating willingness to use force to enforce the rules of the existing system.

INSTC & BRI Disruption

Strikes on Bandar Abbas (Iran's main port) specifically targeted the International North-South Transport Corridor — the Russia-Iran-India trade route designed to bypass Western-controlled maritime chokepoints. This corridor, along with the Belt and Road Initiative's Iran segment, was critical to the de-dollarization infrastructure project.

The Rare Earth Angle

China controls 85–90% of global rare earth processing. Trump's Jan 2026 critical minerals EO, Ukraine minerals deal, and Greenland push are attempts to build alternative supply chains before the US-China rivalry peaks — ensuring the US retains technological and military dominance regardless of how the trade relationship evolves.

🛢️ Oil Markets — Risk Premium vs. Oversupply Tug of War

The Bull Case (Conflict Scenario)

Brent crude moved from ~$70 to $80+ immediately following the Feb 28 strikes. Kpler and Morgan Stanley models suggest a surge to $130/barrel if Iran retaliates by closing or threatening the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20% of global oil supply transits daily. Iranian strikes on Saudi Arabian and Qatari energy infrastructure are already being modeled as tail risks.

The Bear Case (Oversupply Baseline)

J.P. Morgan's base case is Brent averaging ~$60/barrel in 2026, citing OPEC+ oversupply as a structural ceiling. The EIA projects $55/barrel. Trump's "drill baby drill" policy added US supply; OPEC+ members (notably Saudi Arabia and UAE) have been compliant with production discipline but have capacity to add barrels quickly if prices spike.

The hedge fund play: Global macro funds have been running long oil futures as a conflict tail-risk hedge rather than a directional bet — essentially cheap insurance against a Hormuz closure scenario that would be catastrophic for global inflation. The asymmetry is attractive: if conflict stays contained, they lose a small premium; if Hormuz gets blocked, they make multiples.

Energy Equity Rotation

ExxonMobil (XOM) and Chevron (CVX) are up 11%+ since escalation began. The rotation from tech-heavy growth into energy and defense mirrors the playbook seen during Gulf War I (1990–91) and Operation Iraqi Freedom (2003). Airlines, consumer discretionary, and EM equities are the underperformers — facing dual headwinds of fuel costs and risk-off sentiment.

🔮 Private Markets — The Divergence Signal

Hedge Funds — All-In on Volatility

Hedge funds generated an average 11.8% return in 2025 and enter 2026 with record gross leverage. 64% of allocators plan to increase hedge fund exposure — translating to an estimated $24B in additional net inflows. Global macro and commodity-focused funds have been the top performers, benefiting directly from the geopolitical volatility regime.

Goldman Sachs prime brokerage data shows net leverage near 3-year highs, suggesting institutional conviction — not just momentum-chasing — behind current positioning.

Private Equity — Pulling Back

In a striking divergence, net interest in private equity and venture capital has fallen to multi-year lows, with private credit softening as well. The Amundi 2026 hedge fund barometer notes this is the sharpest PE-vs-hedge-fund sentiment gap in years.

Interpretation: long-horizon illiquid capital is unwilling to underwrite the geopolitical uncertainty premium. Investors want liquidity and the ability to react quickly — exactly what hedge funds offer that PE does not.
